Thus, long answer short, we don't reject it, we reject it for that specific initial condition.

When you solve a quadratic in time, say

$$t^2−5t+6=0⇒t=2,3$$

both are mathematically valid. But suppose your problem says:

“A particle is thrown at t=0 - when does it reach height h?”

Now time is being measured from a chosen origin (the throw). So:

- t>0: after the event (future relative to launch)
- t<0: before the event (past relative to launch)

So when you get a negative root, it usually means that this event also occurred before you started observing.

Example: A ball passes a certain height twice:
- once going up (before peak),
- once coming down (after peak).

If you define t=0 at some arbitrary moment (say release), one of those times might fall into t<0.

So the deeper truth is that time isn’t “forbidden” to be negative our chosen coordinate system forbids it for that scenario.
Physics uses reference frames and origins. Time is just a coordinate like position.

You can redefine time:

$$t′=t−t_0$$

Now something that was negative might become positive.

# What about imaginary time

The fact that $t = 2s$ and $t = 2i s$ are 2 completely different ideas, the latter not being a physical time in classical mechanics, it shows up only in advanced physics about which I have yet very limited knowledge.
However, citing some references that I found, imaginary time shows up in advanced physics, especially in quantum mechanics, relativity and cosmology.

For example, in relativity:

$$s^2=x^2+y^2+z^2−c^2t^2$$

Mathematically, people sometimes write:

$$t→iτ$$

to make spacetime look like ordinary geometry. But this is a mathematical trick, not literal time becoming imaginary.

Even in quantum mechanics, when you see something like:

$$e = iωt$$

the i is encoding oscillation not saying time is imaginary.
